Key finding: Unlike prior automation waves that concentrated risk in routine manual work, GenAI exposure peaks in high-wage, high-education white-collar roles — financial analysts, data scientists, writers, and insurance underwriters all score above 0.70. This inverts the historical pattern where technology displaced low-wage workers first. However, exposure ≠ displacement: productivity augmentation may raise output per worker rather than reduce headcount.

Key finding: The 2022–2023 tech layoff wave coincided with — but preceded — large-scale GenAI deployment. The primary drivers appear to be over-hiring during 2020–2021 and rising interest rates compressing tech valuations. However, the composition of cuts matters: roles in content, data annotation, and coding support (all high GenAI exposure) were disproportionately targeted in 2024+ announcements.

This creates a dual risk: displacement would disproportionately destroy high-paying jobs, compressing the middle of the wage distribution from above rather than below — a novel polarization dynamic distinct from prior automation waves.

However, the Information sector shows elevated layoff rates relative to other sectors since late 2022, while overall openings remain high — suggesting a sectoral reallocation rather than broad demand destruction. This is consistent with "frictional displacement": AI substitutes some roles while creating demand for others, with a transition gap in between.

Key finding: The investment-to-employment ratio in AI-adjacent sectors has become less efficient over time — each billion dollars of AI investment creates fewer observable tech jobs than in prior technology cycles. This is partially explained by the fact that GenAI is primarily software infrastructure, requiring limited incremental headcount once deployed. However, Acemoglu's (2024) "simple macroeconomics of AI" cautions that only 4–9% of tasks are truly exposed to cost-effective AI substitution within a decade, suggesting the displacement risk may be overstated in the near term.

Employment and wage data are from official BLS surveys with standard sampling error. Causal identification of AI effects is confounded by macro cycles and tech-sector over-hiring in 2020–2021. This dashboard is exploratory/descriptive, not causal inference.
